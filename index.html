Wet Driving Mode

September 2020

Automotive inclement weather detection system.

As a part of my dev challenge for the McMaster EcoCar Challenge, my dev challenge team and I created an inclement weather-detection system. There are two main parts of this system – a windshield visual rain sensor and a physical water sensor.

The windshield visual check is based off the concept of total internal reflection, in that an infrared light is projected into the windshield and then the critical angle between the infrared light and the car’s windshield is measured. The different measurements provided by this corresponds to different weather conditions because, the critical angle will be different depending on if the windshield is clean or has anything on it, such as rain or snow.

The physical water sensor is mounted in the car’s wheel well and signals that there is water on the road when it detects any water. When no water is detected, the dry mode is active. When the sensor detects water for more than a few seconds (a buffer is implemented to avoid excessive amounts of false positives), wet mode is activated.

  • Languages Used: C#, C, C++, Python

    Lamborghini Huracán

    August 2019

    Diagnosing and fixing unusual issues.

    Right after passing 20,000kms on the odometer and right after exhausting the factory warranty some concerning issues had arisen. The car started throwing fault codes pertaining to the front lift system, emissions systems, and catalyst system. On top of that, the battery had either gone bad or there was some sort of parasitic battery drain (these cars are known to have a lot of parasitic draw), and the car stopped recognizing the key FOB. One or more of these issues was preventing the car from starting. To remedy this, a series of ECU resets were performed to clear the codes (my on-hand OBD II scanner could not clear these fault codes) and the battery was connected to a trickle charger.
